Title: Adaptation V

Artist: Joshua Schicker

Date: unknown

Article Author: Michael G.

This work, intended for home d√©cor, calls to the one bearing witness, and begs them to observe the internal as if it were the obvious external. The quick broad black brush strokes covering this canvass give the faint suggestion of a face to the viewers. This face, empty save for large swaths of blue paint reveals the normally hidden section within the head, or more specifically the mind, of the character depicted. It is a frame within a frame and questions the nature of art in the same manner as a picture drawn within a picture. The concept of what is inside and what is outside, included, excluded, and everything in between runs deep within this piece.

The large blue planes running perpendicular to each other and intersecting within this frame of mind give us the impression that we are seeing the thoughts of this abstract entity occurring in real time. The intersections of their ideas displayed in front of us for our own judgement unbeknownst to the subject. With a voyeuristic and intimate approach, the viewer is shown a privacy we all accept as a divine right: Our internal thoughts. A truly amazing piece in this age of social media, where sharing our opinion is so common place, but the hidden internal layers we keep to ourselves persevere.

Title: Drill

Artist: unknown

Date: 1/11/21

Article Author: Elizabeth C.

This piece plays on the familiar idiom "you know the drill" usually meaning "you are aware of the processes involved." The shy glance at an anti-joke, the The unnamed work reminds of the classic Rene Magritte, or the "This is Not a Pipe." One could say "This is not a drill...or is it?"

Instead of assuming the audience is known by the piece it asks us to self question. Cliche as it may be this work asks us directly "who are you?" A final note on the background, the well known windows background, yet another switch back to the past and the familiar. There exists something both surreal and real in this work of art, something comforting and discomforting. But doesn't this fulfill the purpose of art anyway? Asking the audience for self reflection while both drawing us in and pushing us away? This reviewer thinks so.
